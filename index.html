Dos países, dos contextos operativos, dos idiomas. Y sin embargo, el mismo deseo: una fuente de conocimiento institucional que sea confiable, trazable y que hable el idioma del trabajo real.

Lo que emerge no es un problema de tecnología ni de acceso. Es un problema de confianza epistemológica: los equipos del BID ya usan IA todos los días, pero no confían en ella para documentos oficiales. La plataforma tiene una ventana de oportunidad precisa — no competir con Copilot en velocidad, sino ganarse un lugar donde Copilot no puede entrar: el conocimiento institucional citado, curado y trazable.

03

What diverges — each country has its own pain

Colombia · Unique tensions

Knowledge Careers gap. KM work isn't valued or visible. Incentives are misaligned with tacit knowledge production.

TdR as the most urgent use case. Centralized access to similar terms of reference — concrete, solvable today.

Redactar POD as #1 opportunity. Colombia wants generation more than search — already trusts enough to want outputs.

Brasil · Unique tensions

Portfolio intelligence demand. Cross-sectional data — disbursements by project, risks by region — built manually in Excel today.

PCR automático — explicit demand. "Why isn't there a system that automatically generates a PCR?" Not better search; automation of closure.

Consultar políticas as #1. Brasil prioritizes regulatory evidence over generation — one step earlier on the trust curve.

04

Design implications — what to do with all this

Trust architecture

Design for the citation, not the answer

Every result must show its evidence chain visibly and clickably: source doc, section, date. Not as collapsed metadata — as part of the result itself.

→ Trust isn't built with disclaimers — it's built with radical source transparency.

Positioning

Don't replace Copilot — occupy where it can't go

Copilot has speed and familiarity. The platform has the BID's cited institutional knowledge. Value prop: real bank documents, specific operations, policies with page numbers.

→ The platform wins in the margin where Copilot falls short: institutional precision.

Differentiated roadmap

Colombia wants to generate. Brasil wants to verify. Design for both.

Adaptive UX that calibrates autonomy level per user profile and task type. There's no single use mode — there's a trust progression the UX must support.

→ One platform, two entry points on the trust curve.

Tacit knowledge

Capture what's in no document

An ingestion channel for tacit knowledge: mission notes, workshop minutes, post-operation learnings. Value grows every time a team deposits knowledge.

→ The platform's value is cumulative — each contribution makes it more valuable for everyone.

Quick win · Immediate

TdR and similar operations: the clearest first use case

Finite, definable, demonstrable — ideal for a first version that builds trust. A narrow excellent launch beats a broad average one.

→ Start narrow, prove value, then expand.

Long-term vision · Brasil

PCR automático: the most ambitious and legitimate bet

Capturing execution changes and synthesizing them at closure solves a pain point no other tool addresses. Not a feature — a value proposition that redefines the platform.

→ This use case redefines the platform as a production system, not just a search tool.

Design hypothesis · Transversal

The Knowledge Platform doesn't compete in the generative AI market. It competes in the market of institutional trust. Its differential advantage isn't response speed but knowledge traceability — and that's exactly what no generalist tool can offer.

Colombia and Brasil, with distinct operational profiles, converge on the same signal: we don't need more information, we need information we can trust. That is the platform the IDB has the unique opportunity to build.
