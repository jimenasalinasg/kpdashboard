Four countries, four operational contexts — and a consistent signal across all of them. The same desire: an institutional knowledge source that is reliable, traceable, and speaks the language of real work — not the language of the formal archive.

What emerges isn't a technology or access problem. It's an epistemic trust problem. Teams already use AI daily across all four countries. But they don't trust it for official documents. Costa Rica named Claude as the benchmark spontaneously. The question is no longer whether they'll use AI. It's which one, for what, and who governs it.

01

Universal signals — what crosses borders

Trust · Universal

Traceability over speed — always

Colombia: 100%, Brasil: 87%, Costa Rica: 60% prefer slower answers with cited sources. Panamá: unanimous endorsement of all platform concepts in voting — source traceability was a recurring demand in the synergies session. CR is the first country where speed preference appears (40%) — but the majority still demands traceability. "La respuesta muestra la fuente de manera que se puede validar." — CR participant

Tools · Universal

Copilot #1, but Claude is rising

Copilot leads all 3 countries (11 mentions each in CO/CR, 18 in BR). Claude is growing: 3→4→6 mentions across CO, CR, BR. In CR, ChatGPT ties with colegas at 8 — the most AI-diverse tool mix of the three missions.

Adoption · Universal

Complement, not replace — across all 3

0% want the KP as their main tool in any country. CR: 7 complement + 6 find sources = 76% see it as a layer, not a replacement. "Ya lo hace" [It already does it] — one CR participant, when asked what they need from the platform in one click.

🇨🇴 Colombia🇧🇷 Brasil🇨🇷 Costa Rica

Source quality · Universal

Integration #1 need in CR; cited sources #1 in CO+BR

CO and BR rank fuentes confiables y citadas as #1 to switch. CR flips it — integración con herramientas actuales is #1, fuentes #2. CR users are already more integrated in their workflows and want seamless embedding, not just better search.

AI Use · Universal

AI daily: BR 85% · CR 75% · CO 64%

All three missions show majority daily AI use — and 0% "never" in any country. CR and BR are most mature. The benchmark CR named for retention: "Parecerse más a Claude" [Be more like Claude] — CR participant, Mentimeter retention question.

Platform perception · Universal

Prior use highest in CR — and so is utility rating

CR: 46% had used KP before (vs 33% CO, 20% BR) — and 91% rate it useful, the highest across missions. More familiarity → higher perceived value. But bugs in Convergencia integration threaten this trust: projects that don't appear break the adoption cycle.

Prefer traceability over speed. In all 4 missions, the majority chose slower-but-traceable over fast-but-unverified. Panamá endorsed all platform concepts unanimously — the strongest institutional endorsement in the corpus. Costa Rica is the first country where 40% preferred speed — a signal of growing AI confidence, not declining quality standards. Across all three: "El link a la fuente oficial, siempre hago cross check" [The link to the official source, I always cross-check] (CR) · "Que utilice fuentes oficiales" [That it uses official sources] (CR) · "Que la herramienta se use de manera masiva por funcionarios del BID" [That it be used widely by BID staff] (BR). Social proof and institutional provenance are the two trust levers — design for both.

02

Opportunities — where they converge and diverge

🇨🇴 Colombia 🇧🇷 Brasil 🇨🇷 Costa Rica 🇵🇦 Panamá

% of participants who said they would use each feature across Colombia, Brasil and Costa Rica (Panamá voted 100% on top 5 in concept voting but survey data is partial). Ordered by average approval across 3 surveyed missions. Key read: Connect with AI agents is the widest spread (38%→70%→100%) — the clearest signal of growing AI maturity across countries. Detect gaps and Consult policies hit 100% in Costa Rica while remaining strong across all three.

🇨🇴 Colombia

TdR as the most urgent use case. Teams search for Terms of Reference from similar past projects to avoid starting from scratch — no centralized way to find them today.

Draft POD as #1 opportunity. The most generation-forward profile — already trusts enough to want outputs, not just inputs.

Knowledge Careers gap. KM work isn't valued or visible. Metrics reward publications over field execution. Knowledge work happens after hours with no system to capture it.

🇧🇷 Brasil

Portfolio intelligence demand. Disbursement projections, risks by sector/region — all built manually in Excel today. The platform could replace this.

PCR automation — explicit demand. "Why isn't there a system that automatically generates a PCR?" Teams want closure documents generated from execution data, not written from scratch.

Institutional memory dispersed. Lessons learned by state, municipality and execution mechanism aren't accessible — the hardest knowledge retrieval problem in the corpus.

🇨🇷 Costa Rica

"Be more like Claude." A user named Claude as the retention benchmark spontaneously. The platform must win on institutional specialization, not by restricting generic AI access.

Cross-window silos. No structured cross-visibility between BID Invest, BID Lab and BID. Synergies still depend on personal relationships.

Highest prior use (46%) — but Convergencia bugs. Most mature adopter, yet technical failures erode trust. A broken project link undoes months of adoption.

🇵🇦 Panamá

Tools built for HQ, not for executing agencies. The hottest challenge in the corpus (8 reports): specialists can't move the needle because agencies lack access to bank knowledge. Tools are designed for BID staff to report, not for agencies to execute.

PCR is structurally broken — not just slow. The sharpest critique: "One lies in the PCR." The margin of maneuver at closure is 0. PMRs and amendments have more value than the final PCR — the platform should capture the journey, not just the end.

AI governance gap. Panamá was the only country to explicitly ask about AI licensing. The bank is in an "experimentation phase" with no individual licenses yet — users are ready, governance isn't.

04

Design implications — what to do with all this

Trust architecture · All 3 countries

Design for the citation, not the answer

Every result must show its evidence chain visibly and clickably: source doc, section, date. Not as collapsed metadata — as part of the result itself. Costa Rica makes this explicit: "la respuesta muestra la fuente de manera que se puede validar" — and users already do cross-checks before using any AI output in official documents.

→ Trust isn't built with disclaimers — it's built with radical source transparency.

Positioning · Costa Rica signal

Don't replace Copilot — and don't try to replace Claude either

Copilot has speed and BID integration. Claude has conversational fluency. Costa Rica users already use Claude directly (4 mentions) and named it as the retention benchmark. The platform's edge is neither: it's the only tool with cited BID institutional knowledge — real bank documents, specific operations, policies with page numbers.

→ The platform wins where neither Copilot nor Claude can go: institutional precision with provenance.

Differentiated roadmap · 3 trust curves

Colombia generates. Brasil verifies. Costa Rica integrates.

Colombia wants outputs (Draft POD #1). Brasil prioritizes evidence before generating (Consult policies #1). Costa Rica — the most mature adopter — wants the platform embedded in their existing workflow: integration with tools is their #1 switching trigger, unlike the other two missions.

→ One platform, three entry points on the trust curve. UX must adapt to where each user is.

MVP → next expansion

Similar projects is live. Next: exact policy citation + TC contextual search

The MVP already covers similar projects. The clearest expansion validated across all 3 missions: consult policies with exact citation (top-3 in all countries) and deeper TC integration in contextual search — Cooperaciones Técnicas appear as key instruments in Colombia and Brasil but aren't fully surfaced in search results today.

→ Expand from what works. Both next features are low-lift, high-signal for institutional trust.

Cross-window · Costa Rica priority

BID Invest + BID Lab integration: a governance problem, not a technical one

Costa Rica surfaced the sharpest version of this: users from BID can't consult BID Invest environmental and social documents. Synergies between windows depend on personal relationships. This is the use case that makes the platform truly institutional — but it requires cross-team governance decisions before any technical work.

→ Align with BID Invest and BID Lab teams before scoping. The blocker is access policy, not engineering.

Big bets · Align with Project Preparation team

PCR automation + portfolio intelligence: legitimate, scoped, not solo

Brasil named PCR automation explicitly. The vision — capturing execution changes and synthesizing at closure — redefines the platform as a production system. Same with portfolio intelligence (disbursements, risks by region). Both are part of the Project Preparation project roadmap: these field signals should feed that team's prioritization, not be built in parallel.

→ Share these findings with the Project Preparation team. They're building the infrastructure these use cases need.

Design hypothesis · Transversal

The Knowledge Platform doesn't compete in the generative AI market. It competes in the market of institutional trust. Its differential advantage isn't response speed but knowledge traceability — and that's exactly what no generalist tool can offer.

Colombia, Brasil, and Costa Rica — with distinct operational profiles and adoption curves — converge on the same signal: we don't need more information, we need information we can trust, embedded in how we already work. That is the platform the IDB has the unique opportunity to build.
